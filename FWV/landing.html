    1. Whom would you like to thank for teaching you Respect?

      Chanel Perfume Peng: The art teachers at my high school taught me respect. San Francisco is known as an art haven, but my high school is not very enthusiastic about arts education. My school tosses students into random art classes to fulfill their requirements without asking which classes interested them. My art teachers interacted with students because they respected what they wanted and needed. In the beginning of the year they'd always ask whether a student wanted to be in the class so they could help them get into the class they wanted while fulfilling their requirements. If in the middle of the semester, when it was too late to transfer, they would look for a medium that pertained to the class while also providing interest to the student. My visual arts teacher brought in teachers who taught different art forms to show us the possibilities we could explore if visual art wasn't for us. My media arts teacher allowed a talented student to earn their grade by making music even though it was a web design class. Students sprayed graffiti onto canvases instead of walls because an after school teacher provided it for them. My visual arts teacher was laid off. My media arts teacher has lost funding. The after school program was closed for a year. The art teachers do not get the same respect they've provided for students. Art classes are decreasing and supplies get stolen every year, but they keep giving respect by the handful. They've taught me to keep giving respect even when there is no respect given to you. Even though I've already graduated I am still concerned about art in my high school. If I win, I'd like to give the money to my high school, but on the condition it goes towards arts education.

    3. Whom would you like to thank for teaching you Respect?

      Summer Owens: I would like to thank my son for teaching me the true meaning of RESPECT. When I became a mother at age 15 after a forced sexual encounter, I was down and depressed and unsure about my life and my future. The experience of being a pregnant teenager and raising my son alone made me stronger and helped me to learn to respect myself. More importantly, my son and the beautiful challenges I faced raising a young man also gave me a new respect for teen mothers, single mothers, children growing up without one of their parents, parents in general and people with all types of challenges. Because of the respect I learned and had for others, I founded The S.O. What! Foundation which helps people eliminate excuses and overcome their obstacles. We teach people to say, “So what!” to their circumstances (insecurities, weight, teen pregnancy, single parent homes, bullying, whatever!) and say, “so Now what?” to either accept those things they cannot change or take steps to change those things they can. My son has lived his life not only without his father, but also knowing his father raped his mother. He is intelligent, smart, caring, and strong. He is the driving force behind The S.O. What! Foundation as we work to instill the RESPECT he unknowingly taught me in others. For more information on The S.O. What! Foundation, visit http://summerowens.com/thesowhatfoundation.htm

    9. Whom would you like to thank for teaching you Respect?

      Stacia Pombrio: I would like to thank Kim Evans for teaching me respect. She may not remember me, but something she once said will stick with me forever. I went on a service trip to Philadelphia in 2011 with a group called HOPE youth corps (HYC). Together we reached out to the inner city children of the Philadelphia community. Each of us mentored a child, dedicating our efforts to showing them love, friendship, and a positive example. The young girl I mentored had my heart. However, there came a point in our service when we invited the children to sleep over at the camp where we were staying. Suddenly we were with them constantly. Rather than viewing the time as a joy, we began seeing it as a burden. We wanted time alone. We were exhausted and overwhelmed with the different moods of the children as they, too, became tired from the long days. Finally, Kim Evans seated the girls of our group down. She proceeded to open my eyes to the reality of my life. For some reason I live in privileged circumstances. But it could have been me in the shoes of these children. Kim grew emotional, drawing our attention to the difficulties so many of these young children endure. Consequences of drugs, abuse, and violence are just a few of the daily battles they suffer. For some reason, that is their life and not mine. By the time Kim finished, I was in tears. I cannot let my privileges blind me. The children we were serving deserved my respect. I decided to change. If Kim had never opened her mouth and corrected our group, I may have continued withholding respect from those who deserve it most. But as it is she has inspired my future and changed my life.

    15. Whom would you like to thank for teaching you Respect?

      Brenda Wherry-Davis: The person I most would like to thank for teaching me the true meaning of RESPECT is my father John Lee Wherry. Although my father passed when I was 4 years old, I have fond memories of him. You may ask the question, how can someone teach a 4 year old about RESPECT. Well in spite of the illness my father suffered from serving in the United States Army, World War II, he was always sick. One day when he was feeling his lowest he promised me that he would take me to the park and push me in the swing. Many days went by and all of a sudden he pulled his self up out of the bed and said come on we're going to the park. I was so excited I didn't know what to do. I remember my mother saying John you need to be laying down and his reply was no I promised baby girl that I would take her to the park for days and today is the day. My mother did everything she could to convince my father to lay back down, but he wouldn't. We made it to the park and had a wonderful time. My father pushed me in the swings and I remember saying higher, higher. We both laughed. How you might say is this RESPECT. Well my father respected his promise of telling me we would go to the park and we did. What I learned down the line was that loving someone is also a sense of respecting them. I have kept this with me up until adulthood. I have taught my children to RESPECT others. Be a woman and man of your word. RESPECT is the key word to others respecting you.
